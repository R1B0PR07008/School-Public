Should GMO products be labeled? why?
 US:
    In the US certain types of GMOs have to be labeled. They are any and all foods that humans eat that have been genetically modified in ways that can not be replicated through conventional breeding.
 EU:
    Any food that is more than 0.9% genetically modified food has to be labeled in the EU. (Traceability and Labelling, n.d.)
 CR:
    It is written in the Political Constitution of Costa Rica that “Los consumidores y usuarios tienen derecho a la protección de su salud, ambiente, seguridad e intereses económicos; a recibir información adecuada y veraz; a la libertad de elección, y a un trato equitativo. El Estado apoyará los organismos que ellos constituyan para la defensa de sus derechos. La ley regulará esas materias.” (S-COM: Davinsson Nunjar Flores, n.d.) Thus, since it is a constitutional right of the citizen of Costa Rica to know what they are eating, all GMOs in CR should be appropriately labeled.
  Our Opinion
    GMO Product should definitely be labeled. The user has the right, at least here in Costa Rica they do, to know what they are eating and where it came from.

Should some GMOs be allowed but not others?
  No, not all GMOs should be allowed. GMOs that are bad, or have the capability to harm humans or animals in any way, shape, or form shouldn't be allowed. But GMOs that have the capability of making our proceses more efficient, or GMOs that can save the world should definitely be allowed.



Are GMOs a viable solution to World Hunger?
  Arguments in support:
    1. Higher Crop Yield: By engineering GMOs to produce more, more food may be produced on the same area of land. This might assist in addressing the rising food demand brought on by population expansion.
Enhanced Nutrient Content: Through genetic modification, crops can have a higher nutritional content, giving populations vital vitamins and minerals that their diets may be deficient in.
  Arguments against it:
    1. Environmental worries: There are concerns regarding the potential problems of GMOs on the world, including the increase of resistance and the unanticipated effects of genetic manipulation.
    2. Health Risks: Some individuals are worried about how eating GMOs may affect their health in the long run. GMOs have been shown safe in most scientific investigations, although distrust persists and additional study is still needed.

What are some ethical considerations regarding GMOs?
  Effect on the Environment:
   Biodiversity: There are concerns that GMOs can affect biodiversity unintentionally. Genetically modified crops have the potential to cause an imbalance in the ecosystem or drive out native species.
   Gene Flow: Concerns about unprecedented repercussions and the potential for superweeds or superbugs are raised by the idea of GMO genes passing into wild or non-GMO crops.
  Human Well-Being:
   Allergenicity: Concerns have been raised over the possibility that genetic engineering could add new allergens to food, which could have an impact on alergic people.
   Toxicity: There's a worry that eating GMOs could have unintentional negative impacts on the health of the consumer.



What are GMOs?
  GMOs are Genetic modified organisms, which mean that they are living entities with DNA that has been changed in a way that does not occur normally within the species. The method of producing genetically modified organisms (GMOs) requires modifying an organism's DNA in a lab to produce the wanted modifications. GMOs are commonly developed in agriculture and food to raise crop surplus, boost resistance, improve the product’s nutritional value, or make the harvest more resilient to the environment. To quote: “recently developed and newly emerging genetic manipulation technologies, such as recombinant DNA (rDNA), recombinant RNA (rRNA) and cell fusion, that are sometimes referred to as genetic engineering.” (Executive Office of the President & Office of Science and Technology Policy, 1986)
